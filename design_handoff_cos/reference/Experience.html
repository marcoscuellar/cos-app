Three rules for the experience

Everything below obeys three rules. They're how we keep the architecture from leaking into the interface.

  • Projects are what the user sees. No "threads," no "events," no "context objects." The user works with Projects, Ideas, and People — concrete, warm, familiar nouns. The clever model stays backstage.
  • The user never faces a blank screen on return. Every surface opens already answering "where was I?" Continuity is the default state, not a feature you go find.
  • One obvious next move, everywhere. Every screen ends in a single recommended action. The user should never have to decide what to decide.

What the user sees
A greeting, the chat box, and resume cards first — then projects in motion, ideas brewing, what needs attention, recent activity. Newest and most-resumable at the top.
What the user clicks
Continue → drops them back exactly where they stopped. The chat box to ask or capture. A project pill to jump in. An attention item to resolve it.
How they navigate
From here, everything is one click: into any project, into ideas, or straight into conversation. The homepage is a launchpad, never a place you get stuck reading.
How they continue
The two resume cards are the continuity feature. They restore your last focus and offer the next action — no hunting, no reconstructing.
One product decision for you

You called this the homepage; functionally it's "Now." The win is that it's cross-project by default — you see where you left off across everything, not a folder you must pick first. Projects stay the thing you click; "Now" is just the room you walk into.

Decision needed: is the chat box a command bar (ask & navigate) or also capture (drop a thought that gets filed later)? My recommendation: both — one calm input, two jobs.

What the user sees
Project name, a one-line why it matters, a status pill — then the Current Context card: current focus, recent progress, blockers, open questions, and a highlighted next action.
What the user clicks
The tabs to move between facets (Overview, Research, Knowledge, Files, People, Decisions, Timeline, Activity). The Start → button to act. The scoped Ask to query just this project.
How they navigate
Left rail jumps between projects. Within a project, tabs are the only navigation — flat, no nesting, no folders. Current Context is always the default tab.
How they continue
Recent progress restores momentum; the next action removes the "what now?" gap. They can act in one click or ask the project a question in plain language.
Where I'd hold the line

Ten tabs is a lot. Most will sit empty most of the time, and a row of empty tabs reads as homework. Show only the facets that have content — a project with no decisions yet simply doesn't show a Decisions tab. The structure exists; it just doesn't nag.

Recommendation: Current Context is permanent; every other tab appears only once it has something in it.

03 Re-entry Experience

The moment of return,
handled for you.

When you come back to something after a gap, COS meets you with a short welcome-back panel instead of a cold screen. How much it says scales with how long you've been gone.

Depth scales with absence

Same panel, three intensities. The system reads how long you've been gone and says only as much as you need.

~3 days
Nudge. One line — "here's where you stopped" + the next action. You remember the rest.
~3 weeks
Briefing. What changed, open loops re-surfaced, the why restated, next action offered. (Shown above.)
~3 months
Reconstruction. Full re-onboarding — identity, the story so far, current state, and "do you still want this?"
What the user sees
A calm panel at the top of the project (or homepage): time away, what changed, where you stopped — never the raw, cold screen.
What the user clicks
Pick up where you left off → restores their last focus. Or Just browsing to dismiss and look around freely.
How they recover context
The panel is the recovery — it answers the five questions before they're asked. No digging through history to reconstruct the thread.
How they continue
One button drops them into the exact next action. Re-entry ends where work begins.
The humane half

After a long absence, every panel offers a second door: "Still want this?" Archiving a dormant project should feel as light as resuming one. Returning isn't only about picking things up — it's permission to put them down without guilt.

How an idea moves

SparkBrewingExploringTestingReady→ Project

Sparks are unlimited — capture freely. Only Brewing and Exploring count toward the limit of three, because those are the stages that cost real attention. When an idea earns it, Ready → Project graduates it into the main workspace.

What the user sees
Three active idea cards, each with why it matters and its stage. Below: a loose pile of sparks waiting their turn.
What the user clicks
Analyze to reveal heat/momentum on demand. Promote an idea to the next stage, or graduate a Ready idea into a Project. Shelve to retire one.
How they navigate
Ideas is one top-level destination. An idea opens to its own small page (why · status · open questions · related projects) — the same shape as a project, lighter.
How they continue
Returning to a brewing idea triggers the same re-entry panel — "here's where this idea was going" — so even half-formed thoughts are resumable.
The exit that keeps the incubator clean

Shelving an idea asks one question — "what did you learn?" — and saves the answer as Knowledge before it goes. Ideas get a dignified death with a takeaway, so the incubator never becomes the hoard you're escaping. The "max 3" is framed as focus, never a quota.

05 Project Lifecycle (as experienced)

Projects breathe.
Dormant isn't dead.

A project the user hasn't touched in weeks isn't a failure — it's resting. COS shows lifecycle as a quiet status, never a guilt-trip, and makes both reviving and retiring easy.

What the user sees
A small status pill on every project. Dormant projects gently recede rather than flashing red. The story is calm, not anxious.
What the user clicks
Pick it back up, Let it rest, or Archive. Three honest choices — no project is ever trapped in a guilt-loop of overdue badges.
How they navigate
Dormant projects leave "in motion" but stay one search away in Memory. Nothing is deleted by neglect; it just quiets down.
How they continue
Reviving a dormant project runs the deepest re-entry tier — a full reconstruction — so a 3-month gap still resumes in seconds.
06 Information Architecture & Navigation

A thin spine.
Three doors.

The whole app is reachable from a quiet left rail and one always-present input. Three primary destinations; everything else is found by searching or by following a project — not by hunting through menus.

Now · Home
— continue where you left off —
ProjectsGLVE · COS · Ōllin · Recruiting OS · Personal Brand
Ideasbrewing, max 3 active
Ask — always present (ask · capture · navigate) Memory — search everything (people · knowledge · decisions)

People and Knowledge are not top-level menu items — they're reached through a project (the People and Knowledge tabs) or through Memory search. This keeps the spine to three doors and protects the calm. Every screen also carries the omnipresent Ask input, so the user can navigate or capture from anywhere without returning home.

What the user sees
A slim left rail: Home, a short Projects list, Ideas, Memory — and a pinned Ask field at the top. Nothing else competes.
What the user clicks
A project to enter it; Home to return to Now; Ask to talk or capture; Memory to find anything by name, time, or person.
How they navigate
By meaning ("my GLVE stuff"), time ("last week"), or person ("this client") — the three ways anyone actually looks for their own work. Never by folder or path.
How they recover
If they can't remember where something lives, they don't browse — they Ask, or search Memory, and COS brings the thread back with its context attached.
Decisions before we design screens
Approve the structure, and Phase 3 becomes the visual design.
  • Is the chat box on Home a command bar, a capture box, or both? (I recommend both.)
  • Do project tabs hide when empty, or always show all ten? (I recommend hide-when-empty.)
  • Is "Now" the right name for the homepage, or do we keep calling it Home?
  • Three primary destinations (Home · Projects · Ideas) — does that feel too lean, or exactly right?

Once these are settled, Phase 3 is the part you've been waiting for: the white-canvas, Neue-Haas, editorial visual design — and a clickable prototype of these exact six surfaces.
