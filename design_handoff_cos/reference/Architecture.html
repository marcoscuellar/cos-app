01 Product Thesis

COS holds the state of your
mind between sessions.

The scarce resource is not information, storage, or even time. It is re-acquisition of context — the mental cost of rebuilding where you were every time you return.

Every existing tool optimizes the wrong half. Notion, Drive, Obsidian, Asana — all excellent at putting things somewhere. None of them help you return. The expensive moment is never "I lost the file." It's the morning after a week away, looking at a project you know is yours, unable to reload the thread into working memory.

So the thesis: COS is the cache for your working memory. It remembers the live state of each thread you care about and hands it back to you intact, instantly, on return — scaled to how long you've been gone. Its job is continuity. Its only metric is the cognitive cost of re-entry.

The one-sentence thesis
COS is a continuity layer that lets you return to any project, idea, or relationship after any gap and resume in seconds — without reconstructing it from scratch.

Remember what matters · reconnect to where you left off · continue without starting over.

Where I push back — the name

"Operating System" is a great brand and a risky promise. A real OS schedules processes, allocates resources, and brokers everything that runs. COS does none of that — and if we let the metaphor lead, we'll over-build (workflow engines, automations, an app platform) and lose the calm. What you've actually invented is closer to a context cache or a re-entry layer.

Recommendation: keep "COS" as the name and the ambition, but internally hold the discipline that we are building one capability — context recovery — not an operating system for your whole life. Every feature must pass the test: "does this lower re-entry cost?" If not, it's scope creep wearing the OS metaphor.

02 Core Object Model

Six nouns is close.
Here's the fix.

Your proposed objects — Project, Idea, Person, Note, Knowledge, Decision — are mostly right. But a flat list of six hides the two relationships that actually make COS work. Objects aren't peers; they live in tiers, and one of them isn't stored at all.

Tier 0 — The stream (the source of truth)Everything else is derived from this
EventAn atomic timestamped "something happened." The molecule of continuity.
CaptureRaw input: a voice memo, a thought, a meeting. Your "Note", reframed as an inbox state, not a filing destination.
Tier 1 — Containers (where attention lives)Hold a goal and gather everything related to it
ProjectA live context container with a goal in motion. The center of gravity.
IdeaA pre-project container. Same shape, lower commitment — incubation, not execution.
Tier 2 — Crystallizations (what thinking produces)Deliberate, durable outputs — created by the user, not auto-captured
KnowledgeA distilled conclusion. What you learned and why it matters.
DecisionA choice made, with reasoning, so it's never re-litigated.
Cross-cuttingBelongs to no single container
PersonA human attached to many projects, carrying the state of your relationship.
ContextNot stored. Computed on demand from the stream. See §5.
Where I push back — three corrections

1. "Note" is not an object — it's a state. A note is raw capture on its way to becoming something (knowledge, a decision, an event on a project). If we treat Note as a permanent destination, COS rots into the exact junk drawer you're trying to escape. Model it as Capture → triaged → it becomes another object or an event.

2. The most fundamental object is the Event, and you didn't list it. Continuity is reconstructed from a timeline of what happened. If activity is the raw material for Current Context, then the event stream is the foundation the whole system stands on — more primitive than Project.

3. Context is not an object you store. It is a view computed from events, regenerated every time you look. This is the central architectural commitment of COS. Storing context means maintaining it; maintaining it means it goes stale; stale context is worse than none.

Recommendation: adopt the four-tier model. Promote Event to a first-class primitive, demote Note to Capture, and make Context a computed view — never a stored record.

02 · Re-examined The Primacy Question

Is Project really
the primary object?

You believe Projects are primary because everything attaches to them. You also suspect Context might be the true core, with Projects as just one of its forms. You're circling something real — let me separate it cleanly, because "primary" is doing four different jobs in that sentence.

"Primary" means four different things

The whole confusion comes from one word carrying four meanings. Pull them apart and Project, Context, and Event stop competing — each wins a different axis.

Four senses of "primary"They are not the same question
  • Primary substrate
    What everything is derived from → the Event stream.
  • Primary container
    What things attach to → a Thread (Project is its commonest species).
  • Primary output
    What you actually pay for → Context.
  • Primary surface
    How you re-enter → Now (present-tense, cross-thread).
Where I push back — Project is a species, not the root

Project feels primary because it's the most common container — but "most common" isn't "most fundamental." Make Project the root and three cracks open immediately:

The orphan problem. Capture happens before a project exists. A stray thought, a person you just met, a lesson learned — if everything must live under a project, you force premature filing, which is the precise friction that empties every second brain.

The cross-cutting problem. Your highest-value continuity is often between projects: a Person who spans five of them, Knowledge reused everywhere, "what I'm focused on this week" that cuts across three. A project-rooted tree can't hold these without duplication.

Your own model already leaks. Ideas aren't projects. People aren't projects. You listed them as peers — which means Project was never actually the root; it was just the loudest node.

The more fundamental object: a thread of attention

Under Project, Idea, Person, and Area-of-life sits one shared shape: a continuous line of concern toward something you care about — a thread. Each accumulates events, carries a why, and emits context on return. Project isn't the root; it's the dominant species of the thread.

Threads — one object, several speciesAll gather events · all emit context
ProjectA thread with a goal in motion. The dominant species.
IdeaA provisional thread — concern before commitment.
PersonA relationship thread — your context on a human, across everything.
AreaA standing thread with no end — Health, Family, Personal Brand.

This is a revision of my own §2: I'd filed Person under "cross-cutting." It's cleaner to say Person is simply a non-project thread. "Relationship OS" isn't a feature you bolt on — it's what you get for free the moment a Person is a first-class thread.

Now — is Context the core object?

Your sharper instinct: maybe Context is the real product and projects are just one way it shows up. Yes — almost entirely. But Context is not an object. It's the product's output, and that distinction is load-bearing.

The PIM literature you're reading lands on the same point: the hard problem isn't storage, it's maintaining the connection between information and a future moment of need. That connection — material bound to a future you who needs it — is context. It is a relationship, computed in the present tense, not a record sitting in a table.

Where I push back — don't make Context an "object"

The instant you model Context as a stored object, someone has to keep it accurate — and you're back inside the maintenance trap COS exists to escape. Context can't be the "core object" for the same reason a reflection can't be furniture. It's emitted, not stored.

So the grammar of the system: Context is the verb. Thread is the noun. Event is the substrate. The product is the verb; the navigable objects are the nouns; the truth is the substrate.

The resolution

Three layers, each "primary" in its own sense — and now they cooperate instead of competing.

Substrate
Event
What happened. The source of truth everything derives from.
Container
Thread
A line of attention that gathers events. Project · Idea · Person · Area.
Output
Context
The present-tense state a thread emits on return. The product itself.
What this changes
Context is the product. The Thread is the primary object — and "Now," not the project list, is the true center of the app.

Re-entry — your north star — is inherently cross-thread and present-tense, which is exactly what a Thread+Context model produces and a Project-rooted tree cannot. The homepage isn't "a list of projects." It's Now: the computed context across every live thread, with one recommended action.

One caution — don't over-rotate

This is an argument about architecture, not vocabulary. Never show users a generic "Thread." It's cold, and it would undo the warmth that makes COS feel personal. Internally the root is the Thread; on the surface, users still see Projects, Ideas, People — concrete, familiar, human. Project stays the default word and the most-used hub. We change the foundation, not the furniture.

03 Information Architecture

A graph underneath,
a hub on the surface.

The data is a graph: a Person spans many projects, Knowledge is used by several, an Event can touch a project and a person at once. But a graph presented as a graph is overwhelming — the opposite of calm. So the architecture splits cleanly:

  • Underneath — a graph. Objects link freely. Multi-homing is normal: nothing has exactly one parent.
  • On the surface — a hub. The user always navigates from one thing they're thinking about, and everything related is already gathered around it. The Project is the primary hub; Person and Knowledge are secondary hubs reached through it or through search.

The organizing principle is meaning-first: you navigate by the thing in your head ("my GLVE stuff"), never by location. There are no folders, no paths, no databases exposed. Three retrieval axes cover every "where is…" question:

The three retrieval axesEvery search resolves on one of these
  • By container
    "Where is my GLVE stuff?" → the Project hub gathers it.
  • By time
    "What was I doing last week?" → the stream, filtered by recency.
  • By person
    "What's going on with this client?" → the Person hub.

Cross-project objects need two homes that stay in sync: a global library (all Knowledge, all Decisions, all People) and a local view (the same objects surfaced inside each project that uses them). Same object, two windows — never two copies.

04 Navigation Architecture

Navigation is temporal,
not just spatial.

"Where was I?" is a time question, not a place question. So COS opens on time — a re-entry surface — before it offers places. The spine stays deliberately thin to protect the calm.

The spineThree primary destinations, two always-present surfaces
  • Now — primary
    Home. "What matters right now" + continue where you left off. The default landing.
  • Projects — primary
    The containers in motion. GLVE, COS, Ōllin, Recruiting OS, Personal Brand.
  • Ideas — primary
    The incubator. Capped, deliberately small.
  • Ask — always present
    The chat/command surface. Reachable from anywhere, owns nothing.
  • Memory — always present
    Global search across the graph: people, knowledge, decisions, the whole stream.
Where I push back — fewer doors

Your earlier sketch put People and Knowledge in the top nav. I'd pull them out. Every top-level destination is a small tax on calm and a decision the user has to make. People and Knowledge are facets — you reach them through a project or through Memory search, not from a permanent menu item. Three primary doors (Now, Projects, Ideas) plus an omnipresent Ask and Memory is the whole map.

Recommendation: 3 destinations, not 6. Defend the spine ruthlessly — every new top-level item must displace an existing one.

05 Context Model

What "context" actually is.

Context is the minimal set of information needed to resume a thread without reconstruction. Define it precisely or the product has no spine.

Context has five layers. They map exactly to the five questions every object must answer — which is not a coincidence; the questions are the context model.

L1
Identity — what is this?
A one-sentence standing description. No archaeology.
L2
Motivation — why does it matter?
The reason it exists, so the why survives the gap, not just the what.
L3
State — where does it stand?
Status, blockers, open questions, open decisions.
L4
Momentum — what happened recently?
The last few moves, summarized — your short-term memory, restored.
L5
Horizon — what happens next?
One recommended action. The first domino, pre-placed.

Your proposed Current Context fields — Current Focus, Last Activity, Open Questions, Open Decisions, Blockers, Recent Progress, Next Recommended Action — slot cleanly into these layers. They're the right fields. The architectural question is not what the fields are; it's who fills them.

Where I push back — context must be computed, and it must decay

Computed, not authored. If the user has to write and update Current Context by hand, they won't, and within two weeks every project lies to them. Current Context must be generated from the event stream — assembled, summarized, and re-derived every time it's viewed. The user's job is to do the work and let COS narrate it back; not to maintain a status field.

Time-aware. Context isn't static — its recovery depth should scale with how long you've been gone. Back after an hour? A one-line nudge. Back after three months? A full re-onboarding. A model that returns the same briefing regardless of absence is broken. (This drives §6.)

Recommendation: Current Context is a computed, decaying, time-scaled view over events. Manual fields exist only as seeds (you set the goal once); everything live is derived.

06 Re-entry Model

The signature mechanic.

Re-entry is not a screen. It's a moment that recurs everywhere — opening the app, reopening a project, returning to an idea — and COS treats it as sacred. The user never faces a blank canvas on return.

The re-entry contract

  • Never a cold start. Returning always begins with a warm one.
  • Always answer the five questions before the user has to ask them.
  • Always compute a diff since last seen — what changed while you were away.
  • Always end with exactly one recommended next action.

Recovery depth scales with absence

The "3 days / 3 weeks / 3 months" insight from your notes is the core mechanic. Each tier is a different product behavior, not just more text.

≈ 3 Days
Nudge
You remember most of it. A one-line "here's where you stopped" + the next action. Minimal, gets out of the way.
≈ 3 Weeks
Briefing
The thread has faded. A short narrative of what changed, open loops re-surfaced, the why restated, next action proposed.
≈ 3 Months
Reconstruction
You've effectively forgotten. A full re-onboarding: identity, motivation, the arc of what happened, current state, and a gentle "do you still want this?"

Re-entry runs at three scopes: app-level (the morning open → the homepage), project-level (reopening GLVE), and idea-level (returning to a brewing idea). Same contract, different radius.

Where I push back — re-entry must also let things die

A perfect memory that resurfaces everything recreates the overwhelm you're escaping. Part of re-entry is permission to not return — surfacing "you haven't touched Ōllin in 3 months; archive, or pick it back up?" Forgetting is a feature. The system should make letting go as easy as resuming.

Recommendation: pair every reconstruction with a graceful-exit option. Re-entry is two doors, not one.

Things breathe. Honor it.

Project lifecycle

Projects are not permanent monuments — they have states, and the most important one is missing from most tools: Dormant. A project you haven't touched in weeks isn't a failure; it's resting. COS should treat dormancy as a normal state with dignity, not a guilt-inducing red badge.

Where I push back — two fixes to the funnel

1. The "max 3" should cap active attention, not the whole funnel. If the limit counts every Spark, capture becomes punitive and people stop using the inbox. Let Sparks accumulate freely; cap only the Brewing/Exploring stages at 3. That's a true WIP limit — it constrains focus, not capture.

2. The funnel has no dignified exit. Right now an idea can only succeed (→ Project) or rot silently in place. Most ideas should die — and when they do, the learning should be extracted as Knowledge before the idea is shelved. Add a Shelved / Released exit that asks "what did we learn?" Otherwise the incubator becomes the hoard you're trying to prevent.

Recommendation: WIP-limit the middle, not the mouth; and give ideas a death with a takeaway.

09 AI Behavior Model

A supporting character
with a strict job description.

AI is not the product; it's the layer that makes context computable. Define its posture precisely or it will quietly eat the interface.

Three postures, in order of trustMost work is invisible
  • Ambient (silent)
    Continuously assembles Current Context, summarizes activity, maintains memory. The user rarely sees it work — they just find context already there.
  • Invited (on demand)
    The chat/Ask surface. Answers, recovers, brainstorms — but only when called. Never speaks first here.
  • Proactive (rationed)
    Re-entry briefings, blocker detection, "you haven't touched this." Strictly capped — a budget of nudges, not a stream.

The non-negotiable: legibility

If you can't trust the memory, you can't outsource it. Every AI-generated summary must be traceable to its source events — "why am I being told this?" always has an answer one tap away. An external brain that hallucinates is worse than no external brain, because it corrupts your real memory with false confidence.

Where I push back — the boundary that protects the product

AI assembles and reminds. It does not decide, and it does not author. The moment COS starts making decisions or writing your knowledge for you, the user stops being the thinker and the product becomes "another AI workspace" — exactly what you said COS is not. Brainstorming is allowed, but only when explicitly invited, and the output is always clearly the machine's draft, never silently merged into your memory.

Recommendation: hard rule — AI may summarize, surface, and suggest; it may never decide, file, or crystallize Knowledge/Decisions without an explicit human act.

10 Risks, Blind Spots & Alternatives

The ways this fails.

If COS dies, here is the most likely autopsy — in order of lethality.

  • R1The signal problem — COS isn't where the work happens.
    Existential
    Computed context needs a rich event stream. But the real work happens in code, Figma, docs, calls, your head — not in COS. With thin signal, Current Context degrades into manual upkeep, and then it's just Notion with extra steps. This is the central bet. Three escapes: (a) become the work surface (heavy, fights the calm); (b) become an aggregator that ingests events from the tools you already use; (c) lean on lightweight check-ins — a 30-second daily/weekly "what moved" that's pleasant enough to actually do. My lean: (b) + (c) — integrate where possible, and design check-in as a ritual, not a chore.
  • R2Capture friction kills external brains.
    High
    Every second-brain graveyard has the same cause of death: logging became work. If getting context in costs more than the context is worth, the system empties out. Capture must be near-zero-effort (voice, quick capture, passive ingestion) and triage must be optional, not required.
  • R3Trust collapse from a single wrong summary.
    High
    The first time the re-entry briefing confidently tells you something false, you stop believing all of it — and an external brain you don't trust is dead weight. Legibility (§9) is the mitigation, but the deeper rule is conservatism: COS should under-claim, cite sources, and say "I'm not sure" rather than fabricate continuity.
  • R4The WIP limit reads as punishment.
    Medium
    "Maximum 3 ideas" is the right constraint and the wrong feeling if delivered as a wall. Framed as protection ("focus is the point"), it's a gift; framed as a quota, it's anxiety. Tone is load-bearing here.
  • R5Single-player by conviction — but life is multiplayer.
    Medium
    The whole thesis is intimate and solo, which is a real, defensible position. But projects involve clients, candidates, family. Decide now to be unapologetically single-player and let "People" be your context on others — not shared workspaces. Bolting on collaboration later will distort the architecture; commit to the constraint.
  • R6Blind spot — the product is emotional, not just functional.
    Blind spot
    These aren't "tasks." They're someone's life, identity, and unfinished ambitions. Re-entry after 3 months can carry guilt ("I abandoned this"). COS's tone has to hold that gently — continuity without shame. Most productivity tools fail here by accident; for COS it's central.

Three alternative architectures

Before committing, steelman the roads not taken. Each is a coherent way to build COS; the recommendation is a hybrid.

Alternative A

Stream-first / journal

Everything is a timestamped event in one log. Projects and context are views over the stream. (Think a disciplined Roam / activity-log.)

Strengths

Zero filing. Continuity is native — the timeline is the product. Matches the computed-context thesis perfectly.

Weaknesses

Weak at standing identity ("what is this project, at rest?"). Pure streams are hard to navigate by meaning.

Alternative B

Document-first

Each project is a living document; Current Context is the top section. (A disciplined Notion.)

Strengths

Familiar, flexible, immediately legible. Easy to adopt.

Weaknesses

Maintenance burden — context is authored, so it goes stale. This is the exact trap COS exists to escape.

Alternative C

Agent / memory-first

COS is mostly a conversational agent over a memory store; the UI is chat plus retrieved cards.

Strengths

Lowest capture friction. Matches a chat-first instinct. Recovery feels like asking a person.

Weaknesses

Trust/legibility problems (R3). AI dominates — violates "AI is a supporting layer." Hard to browse.

My recommended architecture
Computed context over an event stream, presented through calm project hubs.

Take the stream as source of truth (Alt A) so context is always computed and never maintained. Present it through project hubs (the legibility of Alt B) so navigation stays meaning-first and calm. Use the agent (Alt C) as a lens over the stream — invited, legible, bounded — never as the whole interface. Crystallizations (Knowledge, Decisions) are the only things the user deliberately authors. Everything else, COS derives.

Open questions for you
Before Phase 2, four decisions only you can make.
  • The signal bet (R1): do we integrate with your existing tools, design a check-in ritual, or both? This is the make-or-break decision and it shapes everything downstream.
  • Single-player by conviction (R5): are you willing to commit to no real-time collaboration in v1, and treat People purely as your context on others?
  • The OS scope discipline (§1): do you accept "context recovery is the one job," and that we'll cut anything that doesn't lower re-entry cost?
  • Capture model (R2): voice-first, quick-capture, passive ingestion — what's the lowest-friction way you personally would actually log what happened?

Answer those and Phase 2 becomes the context model in motion — flows, states, and only then, screens. The goal was never organization. The goal is continuity.
